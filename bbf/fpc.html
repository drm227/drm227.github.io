Boston By Foot

Welcome to the Fort Point Channel tour. We will be talking about the channel, the land and buildings alongside it, and the bridges over it. Today's tour will last 90 minutes, and we will walk a little over a mile. The tour will end at Rolling Bridge Park where Dorchester Avenue crosses the channel.

Boston By Foot is a non-profit educational organization. It was founded in 1976, to promote public awareness of Boston's historical and architectural heritage. We offer a variety of tours of Boston, which are described in the brochure that I gave you (wave it). Boston By Foot has around two hundred volunteer tour guides.

Haunched Girder

The load is transferred from the girders directly to the supporting piers. A haunched girder is deeper at the piers and shallower mid-span typically following a parabolic curve. Haunching increases the stiffness at mid-span to a a graceful aesthetic not found in thicker boxy girder bridges.

The concrete is pre-stressed using pretensioned tendons. Tendons are steel cables pulled tight to form a straight line under high tension. Concrete is then cast around the tendons. The tension on the tendons is released after the concrete hardens. The friction against the concrete prevents the tendons from moving, so the compression force is transferred from the tendons to the concrete. This puts the concrete itself under compression, giving it added strength.

Conduits

The bridge was designed with an above-water utility crossing in mind. There are various pipes under the bridge. Some provide drainage from the roadway and others are rented space for utilities to connect South Boston and downtown.

1930 Bridge

James Michael Curley second stint as mayor; plaque on north side; Joseph Strauss (Golden Gate Bridge); Strauss Bascule Bridge Co of Chicago

Strauss multiple trunnion, overhead counterweight bascule bridge

designed by the Strauss Bascule Bridge Company of Chicago, who designed Golden Gate Bridge

Strauss used concrete for the counterweight instead of iron, thus reducing costs. To allow the great weights to move, he developed the mUltiple trunnion, or pin-connected counterweight, which allowed the counterweight to pivot as it moved, and obviated the need for a pit to receive the counterweight when the leaf was upright.

ornamented by the noted Boston architects, Desmond and Lord ... The cut-stone piers surmounted by ornamental lanterns enhance the setting for the Strauss Bascule, the engineering feature.

South Station

Mayor Josiah Quincy, great-grandson of Great Mayor; Boston Terminal Company (1896); union station consolidating four railroads: NY&NE, Old Colony, Boston & Albany, Boston & Providence

U-shaped, 2190 ft; Atlantic Avenue extended along west side; Summer Street extended along north side; Dorchester Avenue extended on east side on land made on east side by wharfing out; main terminal building extended 700 ft down Dorchester Ave; ancillary building beyond (ice, power, gas); 2400 ft seawall along channel

sections of Federal and Kneeland demolished; existing bridges replaced for realigned tracks and streets

28 platform tracks cover by massive iron and glass train shed; train shed, 602x570 ft, arched roof, 112 ft tall in center; head house plus train shed covered 13 acres; replaced in 1930

New Haven RR bankruptcy 1961; BRA buys South Station in 1965, begins demolition in 1970; bought by MBTA in 1978; restored 1984-89

Summer Street Bridge

the only known surviving electrically-operated, paired-leaf oblique retractile drawbridge

the draw span piers support the draw rails on steel-and-timber runways angled at 45° to the bridge. A timber pile runway pier supports the draw machinery, drum, gearing, and electric motor
