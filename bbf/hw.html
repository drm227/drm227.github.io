Boston By Foot

Scope of Tour

Historic Waterfront ambiguous ... focus on central waterfront ... has moved ... walking current shoreline

two periods of waterfront change ... 19th century shoreline and buildings ... 20th century office and residential space

Colonial Period

cove on eastern side of Shawmut peninsula

small scale development ... buildings constructed by individual owners ... piecemeal landmaking by "wharfing out"

two exceptions ... Barricado 1670's ... Long Wharf 1711-15

19th Century

large landmaking projects move shoreline ... large warehouse blocks with multiple units

waterfront still dominated by maritime activities

20th Century Decline

collapse of regional manufacturing after WWII ... Boston as rust belt city ... wharves and buildings fall into disuse ... some converted to parking lots

end of maritime era for central waterfront

20th Century Redevelopment

Boston recovery from 1960's - 1980's ... current residental and office space created ... mixture of new construction and adaptive reuse

BRA Waterfront Renewal Plan ... extended from Rowes Wharf to Battery wharf (South Battery to North Battery)

Construction

built by Oliver Noyes 1711-15 ... 1600 feet long, extending past Lewis/Rowes line to deep water ... shoreline at time past Custom House

Old Buildings

Gardner Building from 1760 ... Georgian style ... only remaining brick warehouse from before Revolution ... rennovated early 1970's by Anderson Notter

Custom House Block opened 1847 ... designed by Isaiah Rogers ... rented to U.S. Customs ... renovated by Anderson Notter early 1970's

Old State House

completed 1713 ... Georgian style ... seat of colonial government ... lion and unicorn ... view of harbor from Governor's office

view blocked for most of 20th century ... 1901-1941 by Atlantic Ave Loop ... 195?-2004 by elevated highway

Central Wharf & Custom House

completed 1817 ... shore line was India St ... warehouse block ... surviving remnant

Custom House completed 1847 ... Ammi Young ... classical revival ... temple fronts ... on made land between wharves

tower completed 1915 ... cheaper than buying land ... exempt from local zoning law (125 ft height limit) ... tallest building in Boston until Prudential tower

owned by Ted Berenson ... demolished remaining portion of warehouse block in anticipation of residential development ... parking lot until aquarium

NE Aquarium looking for location ... Collins and Logue convinced them to locate here ... opened 1969 ... Brutalist design by Cambridge Seven ... does not take advantage of harbor view

East Boston

no such place in Colonial times ... five islands used by farmers to raise hay and graze livestock

1833 William Sumner forms East Boston Company ... lays out street grid ... starts landmaking

develops quickly with ship building ... population grows from 8 in 1833 to over 15,000 in 1855

Donald McKay and clipper ships ... translantic service to Cunard Line's pier in 1840

Logan Airport a 20th century addition ... opened in 1923 on 134 acres of made land ... now has 1413 acres, mostly from 1940's

FAA height restriction

South Boston

1012 acres made land ... east from Fort Point Channel ... back about a mile to 2nd St

mostly made at end of 19th and beginning of 20th centuries

piers, warehouses, and rail yards ... fell into disuse after WWII ... rail yards converted to parking lots

BRA plan in 1990's ... massive investment ... Turnpike extension and Ted Williams tunnel ... Silver Line ... Seaport Blvd and Moakley bridge ... Moakley Courthouse ... BCEC

building from this century on parking lots that wer previously rail yards

Before Redevelopment

Atlantic Avenue ... Union Freight Railroad ... Quincy Market Cold Storage ... wholesale produce businesses ... T-Wharf

T Wharf built in 1720's ... out of Long Wharf, not shoreline ... fish market from late 1800's to 1914 when Fish Pier opened ... in disrepair with few residents by 1960

Redevelopment

cove concept

move Atlantic Avenue inland ... relocate wholesale produce businesses ... shut down Union Freight Railroad ... demolish T-Wharf

Park

opened in 1976 ... designed by Sasaki ... initially called Waterfront Park

Hotel

opened 1982 ... Post-Modern design by Cossutta and Associates ... harbor end resembles prow of ship

straddles old Atlantic Ave, blocking direct path ... opening a publid way

eight submissions to BRA for hotel ... proposal by developer Zuckerman and architect Cossutta ranked low by BRA ... overruled by Mayor White ... BRA director Walsh resigned in protest
